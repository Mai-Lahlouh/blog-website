Introduction

"Mornings in Jenin", by Palestinian-American author Susan Abulhawa, is an epic, multi-generational novel that brings readers face to face with the tragedy of the Palestinian Nakba. The novel spans several decades, telling the story of the Abulheja family who are displaced from their village in 1948 and forced into the Jenin refugee camp. It is a tale of love, loss, resilience, and the search for identity in a land marked by conflict.

Summary

The story begins in Ein Hod, a peaceful village, where the Abulheja family lives until their lives are shattered during the Nakba in 1948. Forced from their home, they move to a refugee camp in Jenin, where they experience the harsh realities of displacement. At the heart of the novel is Amal Abulheja, whose journey from childhood to adulthood mirrors the struggles of her people.

Amal grows up in a world filled with war, loss, and uncertainty. As she comes of age, she is separated from her family, witnessing the disappearance of her brothers and the death of her loved ones. Despite her many tragedies, Amal’s story is also one of survival and strength. Through her eyes, readers experience the human cost of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, a cost that is both deeply personal and painfully universal.

Themes

  • Displacement and Exile: Abulhawa vividly portrays the emotional and psychological toll of being forced from one’s homeland, as experienced by generations of Palestinians.
  • Family and Identity: The novel is a poignant exploration of what it means to belong, not just to a place, but to a family and a people.
  • Resilience in the Face of Adversity: Despite their immense struggles, the Abulheja family—and Amal in particular—display incredible resilience, reminding us of the strength of the human spirit.

Conclusion

"Mornings in Jenin" is more than just a historical novel—it is a deeply emotional narrative that gives voice to the Palestinian experience. Through its compelling characters and heart-wrenching plot, the novel challenges readers to reflect on the human consequences of war and the enduring hope for peace and justice.
