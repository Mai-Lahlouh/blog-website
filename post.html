Introduction

"Returning to Haifa" is a novella by Palestinian author Ghassan Kanafani, originally published in 1969. This powerful work of fiction examines the experiences of a Palestinian couple, Said and Safiyya, who return to their home in Haifa after twenty years of exile. The novella explores themes of memory, identity, and the impact of the Palestinian-Israeli conflict on personal and collective lives.

Summary

The story follows Said and Safiyya as they return to Haifa after the 1967 Six-Day War, when Israel opened its borders for Palestinian refugees to visit their former homes. Having been forced to flee during the 1948 Nakba, they revisit their house, hoping to find a connection to their past. Instead, they discover that their son, Khaldun, who was left behind as an infant, has been raised by a Jewish family and now identifies as Dov, an Israeli soldier.

This encounter raises questions of identity, belonging, and the ownership of memory. The novella’s complex exploration of the meaning of "home" and "return" speaks to the broader Palestinian experience of displacement and loss.

Themes

  • Displacement and Exile: Kanafani portrays the emotional devastation of being uprooted from one’s homeland, capturing the sense of loss that haunts Palestinian refugees.
  • Memory and Identity: The novella grapples with how identity is shaped by personal and collective memory, and how time transforms relationships with places and people.
  • The Complexities of Return: "Returning to Haifa" challenges the simplistic notion of "return," suggesting that both individuals and places are irrevocably changed by the passage of time and history.

Conclusion

"Returning to Haifa" is a poignant and thought-provoking work that delves deep into the emotional and psychological effects of displacement. Ghassan Kanafani’s novella continues to resonate with readers today, highlighting the enduring pain of exile and the complexities of identity in a conflict-ridden world.
